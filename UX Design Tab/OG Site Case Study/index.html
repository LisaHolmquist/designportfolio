    Portfolio

    arrow_upward

    The Problem:

    I needed to find a way to present my body of work in an appealing, professional, and easy to read manner. However, I didn't have much exposure to the professional world of UX. I was unsure what was expected of a UX portfolio. What was most important to present and how? What did I need to work on to be job ready and have a polished portfolio?

    The Challenge:

    Finding what information is the most important to present to users. What is the primary purpose of the site? What kind of emotion and experience would the users of this site need to experience for this site to succeed in its purpose?

    The Solution:

    I began by researching top rated UX portfolios as well as looking through job listings that I wanted to eventually apply for. I then decided on branding and messaging. This portfolio was later changed due to findings in the curse of the original site's usage in the job search.

    Understanding Needs

    As I began working on my initial portfolio, I felt a great need to understand what information was important to present as well as how to present it. However, I did not have direct contact with recruiters or design managers so I set out to research how to create a portfolio on my own.

    First, I needed to understand what recruiters and design managers were looking for. I sought out job listings that I was aiming to interview for, found the section of essential skills and experience for said jobs, and jotted down various skills and concepts that they were looking for. Two examples of these job listings are shown below:

    After establishing my understanding of what companies were looking for, I set out to understand how portfolios are typically organized. I searched the best rated UX design portfolios and sorted through each listed. What made these portfolios special? How were they presenting their case studies? What was their visual representation to written explanation ratio? What did their general navigation look like?

    I studied two portfolios in particular, that of Olivia Truong and Stephanie Lawrence. The image below is a screenshot of Olivia Truong's portfolio. Her case studies were presented in about 50% imagery and 50% written explanation. I felt that her case studies felt a very linear format and told a compelling design story. I also adored the hand drawn images that she included as she explained her process.

    The image below shows a screenshot from Stephanie Lawrence's portfolio. What I really found compelling about her case studies was how short they were. She established what her plan was and how her research directed her design process and decisions and beyond that, her case studies were presented in a very brief fromat making them very easy and quick to read and understand.

    Branding & Initial Design

    Deciding Overal Message & Style:

    I initially went into the visual design process with the intention of having a very graphic portfolio while remaining professional and conveying key information effectively. Below, you can see my original mood board and UI style guide. I wanted to use some "unconventional" colors. I felt that blue, for example, is used a lot and I really wanted to push my visual narrative into something that feels more fresh and interesting. I also used some feminine typography that I felt was still bold and clean.

    Initial Design:

    When beginning my original design, I took some inspiration from the design sensibilities of Olivia Truong, mentioned earlier. She had very visually appealing imagery for her hero images of each page. They were interesting, graphic, and clean. I wanted to go a slightly different direction but still maintain this graphic feel. I, therefore, went for a more retro visual design, almost like the feeling of a 70's graphic tee. However, after creating this initial design, I felt that it wasn't very representative of the feeling I wanted to evoke visually. I did like the space in the design to keep things simple and clean but ultimately decided to redesign this to create something a bit more graphically striking.

    Final Design

    I pushed and pulled for quite awhile with different styles and imagery. Eventually, I found a couple references of fashion and photography websites that I found very visually striking. With these images and concepts in mind, I designed the homepage found below. The logo of the lotus was a logo I created and used previously for my freelance animation accounts. I felt that it would be important to maintain consistency throughout my online presence and, therefore, added the logo on the homepage as well.

    I went with a similar idea that I had on my original design for the presentation of my body of work. Having the case study title, mock-up example, and link to read more all presented together. In this case I also added a small summary of the case study, my role in it, and the timeframe that it was designed in. Overall, I feel that this page succeeded but maybe could be simplified visually.

    My About page was quite simple with a summary of me and where I came from along with why I am passionate about UX design. However, as shown below, I also added a section that was a graphic representation of my hard and soft skills. Frankly, I feel that this was a poor and inaccurate way to represent skill proficiency and, therefore, did away with this section for my current site.

    Site Analysis

    From the launch of the original site I had Google Analytics attached in the code in order to monitor how users were using the site after I began the application process. I have attached a few examples of the results below. The first one shows the trajectory of users visiting the site. The beginning spike, I decided, is inaccurate as I was ironing out some bugs in the code still so a lot of those user visits were me testing the site on different devices while also filling out initial job applications. At first, I found the number of users dissappointing but I did feel that this showed that my resume potentially needed a little work in order to channel recruiters to my site more frequently. On the right of the graph you can see a small spike in visiting users which was right after I made resume adjustments, showing that my changes to my resume were successful in getting past the initial sorting process of job applications.

    The two graphs above are showing the total number of views for each page of the site as well as how long each user spent on each of these pages. I found this insightful as I found that the great majority of users did go to the work tab but then didn't visit other pages afterword. What does this mean? I felt that this problem could be twofold. First, my body of work may not be interesting to users visiting. Perhaps these are topics that are common among my fellow bootcamp grads. Second, I may not be presenting my work well.

    With these things in mind, I set out to present my work in a more appealing way and brainstorm ideas for new case studies to help me hone my skills and set me apart from other candidates.

    Some of the most useful information, however, came from a few chance meetings with experienced designers in the field. First, I went to a product design and product management conference in Salt Lake City, Utah called the Front Conference. I met so many incredible designers, researchers, and product managers there who gave me a lot of incredible advice and encouragement. A couple things really stood out to me from what they told me. One designer was also a web developer and they told me of something called Hotjar which is a heat mapping tool for websites so I could get even more useful information than I was getting from Google Analytics alone, such as where exactly were the users spending their time on each page. This would enable me to further finetune each page on my site for my specific users.

    I returned from this conference with many new connections and decided to seek out the portfolios of some designers that I was partially impressed with there. I went through each portfolio and took notes of where I thought their portfolios succeeded and where I felt maybe they could have improved. These notes became essential to the redesign of my new portfolio site.

    I also was able to land a second job interview for a design role. This was teh first interview I was able to get and I learned so much from the process. From the various questions they asked I found that my portfolio did not answer a lot of essential questions that it could answer for hiring managers and recruiters. For example, my portfolio at the time did not show a lot of my personality, it didn't show any of my animation work, and it wasn't organized in a way that made it easy for recruiters to find essential information within case studies that they were looking for.

    Resulting Changes

    An even more important question than what did I learn is what did I do with what I learned. After gathering everything I had learned over the months I decided to do a complete revamp of my entire portfolio. I wanted to have a site that was more graphically striking. I wanted to incorporate more hand drawn animations in each page to show a little more of what I am working on in my current role. I combined my animation and UX portfolios, adding an animation tab seen above and added a lot of navigation and scanibility features for recruiters and hiring managers to find exactly what they are seeking. Examples of these changes can be seen below. Please feel free to explore the site as much as you'd like. This site was completely hand coded as teh site before it. A lot of love, care, time, and consideration went into every aspect of this site so please enjoy!
