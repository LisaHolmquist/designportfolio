    Therapy Lab Site

    arrow_upward

    The Problem:

    The current Physical Therapy Lab site has confusing navigation, no clear information hierarchy, and unreadable text blocks. What is most important for users in the site? How can the site help direct the flow of business for Physical Therapy Lab?

    The Challenge:

    Two primary challenges presented themselves in this site redesign. First, what are users' intentions when visiting the site? Where is the knowledge gap, if any, between patients and this kind of physical therapy? Second, what are the business goals of Physical Therapy Lab with regards to this site? How would they prefer to have users utilize the site? How would they like users to be directed?

    The Solution:

    Understand users' intentions when visiting the site. Discover what a user needs to know when considering this kind of treatment. Understand the goals of the business with regards to the site.

    Understanding the Current Site

    Desktop Design Redlining:

    I first began this redesign process by analyzing the current site. I circled areas and annotated possible design concerns regarding each section. I felt this would be helpful for me and my client to review to understand what may be causing user frustration with the site. It also helped facilitate discussion with my client regarding information hierarchy. What was most important/necessary for users when visiting the site? As his site is primarily utilized to inform users on different treatments and services as well as schedule appointments, it was imperative that the site is stripped of anything that is not aimed towards these functions.

    I analyzed and annotated both the desktop and mobile versions of the site to get the full picture of what we were working with. Below you can find the analysis of the desktop version and then the mobile version analysis can be found further down.

    Understanding the User

    After analyzing various design elements in the current site, I felt that the most important thing to understand was the knowledge gap of users when visiting a site for pelvic floor therapy. What information would a user be primarily searching for? What information was important for them to understand before starting treatment? To find this information I began by taking advantage of Google's algorithm. I searched things like "pelvic floor physical therapy" and "what is pelvic floor physical therapy?" I then scrolled down to the google sections titled "People also ask" and "Related questions." This helped me understand some of the information searched by potential patients. A couple screenshots of these results can be found below. This exercise helped me understand how information should be prioritized on the new site.

    At this time I also sent out a survey on Facebook (to target the local audience) to understand what potential users already know about pelvic floor physical therapy as well as what they feel they would need to know in order to start treatment. However, when reviewing the results of the survey I felt that there were not enough respondents (50) to make any decisive conclusions.

    I did, however, feel that I still needed more information resulting from a large pool of people to understand more of why people don't seek pelvic floor physical therapy when needed. With this goal in mind, I went searching for studies with large groups of people and found the results displayed to the right showing that the primary reason patients don't get pelvic floor therapy is feeling uncomfortable due to the intimate nature of the practice.

    I found this information to be key in creating the mood of the overall design concepts to create a 'friendly' and 'safe' design for potential users.

    Understanding Business Goals

    At this point in the process I felt that I understood well enough to present my findings to my client and discuss possible solutions. However, I still wanted to discuss business goals with my client to understand how the website fits into these goals. These would include how the business would prefer patients to use the site. Would they like scheduling to primarily be through the site? Do they prefer to inform patients in person about different services or would they like that information present on the site to facilitate the scheduling of appointments?

    I met with my client, who is the founder and owner of this establishment, to discuss with her the needs of her users as well as her business goals. In this meeting she informed me that she would prefer current and future patients do their appointment scheduling through the site and the site to bridge many knowledge gaps for potential patients to make them as comfortable as possible in their knowledge when seeking treatment. As we discussed these business goals we also discussed various possible solutions for the different needs and goals of the website. We also went over one preliminary design I had created at this point and how it may need to change according to her business goals.

    With all of this knowledge uncovered, I felt I had a good understanding of where the site would need to sit between user and business needs and was ready to begin design concepts.

    Preliminary Designs and Iterations

    Initial Design Concepts:

    My preliminary designs were intended to feel out the general feeling and experience from the business owner. I felt that she knew her brand better than anyone and desired to have a deeper understanding of it in order to design a website that would feel consistent with their business identity. I brought these first three design concepts to a meeting with the business owner to discuss why I chose to design them the way I did and the general feeling I tried to evoke with each design. She then told me what she liked from each design concept and the feeling she tried to evoke within her practive. These designs are shown below:

    First Design Round:

    The business owner told me when meeting over the initial concept designs that they are looking for something that feels friendly, bright, and safe. They are also really partial to the purple in their branding and would like the website's primary color to be the same purple. They informed me that they really liked the overall structure of these designs but told me a few order and image changes that they would like to see. Below you can see an illustration of the design decisions I made on the homepage in the first official round of design as well as two secondary webpage images that I created to craft the desired feeling and experience the business owner expressed.

    Second Design Round:

    After looking over the first design round, the client asked to include specific elements and add them to the next design round. These elements included a photo of one of her employees with her son on the homepage as the hero image, the gif of a running woman found on the original website with a statement, an image of an award they were recently nominated for, and more use of the color purple.

    I felt that this many elements could make the design cluttered but I did my best to still make the design cohesive and clean. My primary goal was still to direct user traffic quickly and easily to the primary purposes of the website, scheduling an appointment, learning about services, and learning a little about the staff to help users feel safe in seeking treatment.

    Final Design

    We officially began development in Squarespace after the second round of design. The client was already using this platform for their current site and felt most comfortable sticking with it to make updating their content easier if necessary.

    The client decided she didn't want the primary navigation icons as quick navigation buttons and only wanted a quick call button alongside the other main navigation at the top of the page. As online scheduling was the primary business goal for the website, I then suggested we use a fixed position for the top navigation to have it scroll with the user. This would help with easy navigation on the main page to replace the previously used icons and make navigation easier on other pages when scrolling.

    Final Thoughts

    Overall, I felt that this was an amazing opportunity and a great learning experience. I learned so many things during this collaboration including:

    1. How to approach small businesses
    2. How to write a simple contract for freelance design and development work
    3. The value of my time as a designer and developer
    4. Making sure I don't overpromise what is achievable
    5. How to balance business goals, user priorities, and design
    6. How to present and talk about different design decisions
    7. Different elements in site design and development that contribute to SEO

    There are a few things that I would do differently both in design and in business looking back on this whole experience and I am so excited to implement all the incredible things I've learned in my next design opportunity.
