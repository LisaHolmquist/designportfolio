    The Problem:

    Information in the Department of Education website is difficult to find as too much information is presented at once and many titles and descriptions do not fit the information given. The site also is poorly organized causing user's to feel overwhelmed when searching.

    The Challenge:

    Who is the primary user of the site? What content on the site is the highest priority for our users? How can we organize so much content to have a smooth user experience while still having all information readily available?

    The Solution:

    Redesign the user interface as well as the navigation system for the Department of Education website in desktop and mobile formats.

    Understanding The Users

    Proto-persona:

    When creating a proto-persona there were three primary user's that came to mind for the Department of Education website.

    1. College students managing student loans - either applying for aid or managing payments of current loans.
    2. Educators looking for local educaiton laws or federal education information.
    3. Government employees that work for the Department of Education.

    Ultimately, the timeline for redesign was limited and I felt college students seeking loan information would compose the bulk of users. With this decision in mind, I created the following persona in order to find a research goal.

    Research & Results:

    Through initial independent investigation, I found that the site had a lot of information for student loans and grants but this information was hidden among information clutter and poor navigation systems. Having found this, I felt it was prudent to conduct user testing of the current user base with the following tasks:

    1. Find information for FAFSA eligibility
    2. Find infromation for FAFSA interest rates
    3. Start FAFSA application

    I conducted five user tests and found the following three redesign priorities:

    1. Reduce the amount of text information presented
    2. Change navigation to key issues easier and more intuitive
    3. Adjust hierarchy and content layout to be more intuitive for users

    User Path:

    Heuristic Evalution:

    I studied and evaluated each section of the user path using a scoring system for heuristics to pinpoint the central existing problems with the current site. Through these scores. I found the essential issues with the site were in its appearance/aesthetics. However, there wasn't a small amount of issues in the content section as well. Due to this, I focused primarily on appearance/aesthetic as well as clear content prioritization of the site.

    Information Architecture

    Card Sorting:

    I first created categories based on the categories that already existed on the website. I then organized the secondary pages and links accordingly. A few components changed categories in the process. However, I felt that this was still confusing and convoluted.

    I then got rid of said categories all together and reorganized the cards once again based on themes that I saw within them. Through this process I found 6 total categories. Once these categories were distinguished, the organization of the various components felt much more sensible.

    The 6 catgeories were as follows:

    1. Fund your Education
    2. Statistics
    3. Laws
    4. Chat/Consultation
    5. Guided Search
    6. Low Priority

    Site Mapping:

    In redesigning the sitemap. I found the "Student Loans/Grants" and "Laws" sections proved the most difficult to design.

    The loans section was difficult to organize as there is a lot of information contained on this page. It is also the highest priority for users as this is the primary reason users use the site. Due to these reasons, I strove to create navigation in this page to be as intuitive and user friendly as possible while supporting our users through such a complicated process.

    The laws section has a great amount of information and jargon. This causes user confusion and stress as they had read through a lot of law titles and descriptions to find what they were looking for. In organizing this content, I first read brief descriptions of each law to discover what they were regarding. I then divided the laws into two categories, laws for students and guardians and laws for educators and schools. Under these two categories, I then organized the laws alphabetically.

    Initial Redesign

    Navigation Design:

    I kept the original Department of Education logo, but wanted the header to display the site title better so I centered the logo and title and shifted the search bar to the far right side. I also shifted the "Information about..." section to the left and adjusted the spacing and font to add some space for the user to breathe and feel less overwhelmed. These changes allowed the space needed to display primary content in a bolder fashion, showing users the site's purpose immediately.

    I designed a lean primary navigation bar with the primary three navigation categories decided during the card sorting and sitemap activities.

    I also added a "Guided Search" option and a live chat feature. These features existed in other areas of the site or secondary sites that the site led to. I felt that these features would be more useful early in the experience rather than later so I added them on the front page as well.

    Desktop Footer:
    In designing the desktop footer. I first studied the current footer as well as footers on sites such as the New York Times and CNN. I did this to find the general pattern of footer navigation and, therefore, decide what needed to be included on the footer of ed.gov. I then used my topics found from card sorting and added them to the footer in a consistent order with decided hierarchy and navigation bar at the top of the site.

    Mobile Footer:
    I first looked over the moble sites of New York Times and CNN just as I did for the desktop footer. However, I felt that these footers did not fit the aesthetic vision I desired in the mobile version of the site. I was seeking sleek, clean, and modern where these sites' footers were bulky and convoluted.

    Due to this concern, I ventured to the site I trusted to have these adjectives apply. Apple. Apple's mobile site's footer was clean, only including tiles but being expandable.     User Testing

    Testing Plan:

    To gain a better and objective perspective, I conducted 5-second testing in which I sought thought processes and commentary of the current site aesthetic as well as the functionality of the current navigation system. I tested each person for both the desktop and mobile versions of the site.

    1. Does the current state of navigation make sense?
    2. What do you think of the aesthetic of the current state of the site?
    3. If you were a potential college student looking for financial aid for your student expenses. where would you navigate?
    4. What further feedback would you like to express?

    Testing Results:

    Final Iterations:

    I pulled six steps from a pdf on the Student Aid website showing how to find funding for a college educaiton. I felt that this information was a high priority and would benefit from being on a secondary page rather than a tertiary one.

    I also added a pdf link on the top right if anyone still wanted it in that format.

    I was given user feedback after the first design that the spacing and imagery caused a lot of confusion in trying to read the steps. With this feedback in mind, I went back and redesigned the steps into a simplified graphic that would be easy to follow while still including limited imagery to bring the user's eye to the tile.

    Next Steps & Final Thoughts

    I would like to have more user testing in the future to explore more design iterations. Specifically, I would like to have user testing with more targeted users such as current college students as well as guardians of current students as these users appear to be the primary users of this site.

    Per my current user testing, I would like to explore the folling iterations as well in the future. These design iterations will take a lot of consideration and time to organize prioritization, organization, and spacing but I do feel they would be worth exploring in future iterations:

    1. More imagery
    2. More information content on the homepage and secondary pages
